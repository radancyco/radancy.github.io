QA Edition

An Overview of Responsive Design and Development

What Responsive Is

Responsive design/development allows designers and developers to craft an experience that can be used across a wide variety of browsers on both desktop and mobile devices.

In order to design a truly responsive experience, our creative teams must keep unknown content scenarios, varying device viewports, and different cross-device user behaviors in mind. The challenge comes in the fact that the site can be viewed on multiple devices, but only has one code base. Users on a laptop will be served the exact same site code as users on a smartphone. This means that designs must never cater to one specific browser, device, or operating system.

On the technical side of the fence, responsive design is comprised of three primary ingredients. These are:

  • Flexible grids
  • Flexible images (or other media)
  • CSS media queries

Media Query Example

Baseline

Our baseline properties come first.

body {

  color: red; /* On small screens, the body text will be red.*/

}

Enhanced

Following the baseline properties are our enhanced properties that will be available when the viewport is 600 pixel or wider.

@media(min-width: 600px) {

  body {

  color: green; /* On larger screens, the body text will be green... */
  font-weight: bold; /* ...and bold */
  text-decoration: underline; /* ...and underlined */

  }

}

There are no preterminded breakpoints (That is adaptive). The flow of content and the flexibility of the grid determine where breakpoints are set. That is true responsive design.

What Responsive Isn't

Now that we understand the basics of responsive design, it is important that we abandon any notion that responsive design is simply about making sites or applications "squishy". There is more to it than that. Not only must these experiences conform to different viewport sizes, but they must also perform well and be just as accessible, pleasant, and as intuitive to use across a now wide spectrum of browsing devices as they are within desktop-only environments.

The success of a responsive experience not only depends on how well we can manage, design, code, and test, but also on how well we can work together as a team. As stewards of our client's brand we have an even greater obligation to ensure that the experience we produce is well received by all, regardless of their technological or physical capabilities. In a way, responsive design is just another form of accessibility, making sites easier to use for individuals who do not have the luxury of a large screen. Given this challenge, the importance of collaboration and good communication throughout any given project cannot be understated.

In the past year, the UID team has made great progress in helping our creative colleagues see past the desktop. The UID team now reviews designs very closely before they are sent to the client for approval, and small screen comps are now a requirement on all responsive projects. Training through the RWD School of Rock sessions have also been instrumental in getting our project managers and designers, here and abroad, up to speed on what it means to design in a multi-device world.

How We Roll: Mobile First

With the ever growing prominence of mobile, TMP now embraces a "mobile-first" methodology that all QA technicians should be aware of. By focusing on the core message of our site, we can then create an optimal and (hopefully) more pleasant experience across all browsers. Small screen size limitations often force us to focus on the core experience as well. This helps keep the brand message consistent across all screen sizes, to focus the experience for users on all devices. This is a good thing, as more and more users have come to expect a certain level of consistency across different devices. A user can start her day off reviewing a job description on her iPhone and return to apply for that job on her desktop browser later on—or vice-versa. The experience is not always a singular one.

Mobile-first and Progressive Enhancement: Perfect Together

With progressive enhancement, we focus on our content offerings and baseline experience first and then introduce a more robust experience on more modern browsers. We also future-proof our work and guarantee that our sites will look and behave just as well on future versions of browsers as they do on current ones.

Mobile-first design and development patterns dovetail beautifully with progressive enhancement, in that we typically begin with our small screen experience, a single column layout for instance, and then expand upon that layout and also introduce design and technical enhancements for browsers on larger screens or with wider feature support. In a world inundated with so many different viewing devices, progressive enhancement is a really smart approach to dealing with the new reality we now live in.

Keep in mind that bigger is not always better. Many smartphones now come packed with many capabilities that are not present on even modern desktop browsers, such as integrated social network share functionality and GPS location.

The Big Payoff

Mobile-first progressive enhancement allows us to reach considerably more users who may be accessing the experiences we build through the thousands of different devices that now exist across the planet. In many developing nations, the only point of Internet access for individuals is a mobile device. The name of the game here is global reach—not rounded corners or drop-shadows .

While aesthetics and Javascript functionalities behind what we produce are certainly important , they should only ever be looked upon as enhancements. Tip the scale too far in either direction and you will have a lackluster experience on your hands. Too much style and scripting will overload a page and overshadow the core content, while too little will minimize the impact of client branding. Good web design and development, responsive or otherwise, is about striking the perfect balance to accentuate our clients' messages and brands—not replace or mute them.

Performance

Performance + Beauty = Overall User Experience

Lara Swanson, Engineering Manager of Performance at Etsy (etsy.com)

Throughout the design and development process, it is very easy to get caught up on aesthetics, flashy technological "widgets", and our own assumptions about how others use the web. Things look beautiful and function great and we pat ourselves on the back for our ingenuity...until we try to load our site on a mobile device, and it takes half a minute for all of those beautiful graphics to appear.

Unbeknownst to many, performance actually starts at the design level. The sky is not always the limit in our canvas, the browser. Heavy designs, extraneous images, scripts, CSS files, and other glut that have no content value can spell certain doom for any of the sites we produce if we are not careful. This is one of many reasons why the UID team seeks to be more proactive in the design process at an earlier stage. There are rules to abide by when it comes to loading assets on a web page, and some designers may not always be familiar with the technical specifications. In the review of designs, we help to steer it in the right direction from a performance (and maintenance) perspective.

If the sites we produce cannot load quickly for users, regardless of device, it doesn't really matter what we put on the page. Sites that take over 4 seconds to load run the risk of losing impatient users, or at the very least introducing them to the brand in a slow, negative light . Site speed is also factored into many search engine algorithms. Ranking can be greatly harmed if one's site takes a considerably long time to load.

Performance has always been important, but the desire has been rekindled due to the rise of mobile. Not all data connections are created equal (3G, 4G, Edge Networks) and, unlike broadband, users have data plans that we must now consider in our work. If I'm a user with only a few megabytes of data allotted to me each month, I'm not going to waste it loading your gigantic banner image slider. I will click away before you drain my data budget.

How Can We Measure Performance Easily?

The UID team always attempts to keep the following two numbers as low as they possibly can be on each page that is developed:

HTTP Requests: The more requests to the server for page related assets there are, the longer a site will take to load. This is called latency. The time it takes to make each request also plays a role in latency (server proximity is also a factor, but we won't get into that right now).

Cumulative Page Weight: Cumulative page weight includes markup, images, video, audio, scripts, CSS, and other assets that contribute to the rendering of the page.

We can easily see what these two numbers are, by opening up our favorite browser's web development toolbar and then monitor network activity when a page loads.

In Chrome, do the following:

  1. Open the developer toolbar by clicking the F12 key or by navigating to the menu icon and selecting Tools > Developer Tools.
  2. In the upper right hand corner, select the gear icon . This will bring up the toolbar's settings.
  3. Select "Disable cache (while DevTools is open)". We always want to ensure that we are seeing all requests, as they will initially be requested from the server.
  4. After closing the Settings panel, select the "Network" tab and then navigate to or refresh the page you are reviewing. You will then be presented with a list of all resources being pulled into the page. The totals at the bottom of the panel.

The Performance Budget

Keeping HTTP requests and cumulative page weight low will have a considerable positive effect on performance and is the easiest thing we can do to keep the experience in check. Therefore, the UI team would like to establish a performance budget for all pages and sites we develop, responsive or otherwise. The numbers we should be striving for are as follows:

HTTP Requests

~ 40 - 50

Cumulative Page Weight

~ 450 - 550 KB

These are not "hard and fast" numbers. Client insistence on a certain branded image or feature may push a site over these limits. However, knowing when a page is coming in over a certain threshold would, at the very least, alert the UI developer to any potential performance problems, and allow us to plan and budget in the appropriate time to alleviate such issues. While UI will be certainly aiming for these numbers during development (and to hopefully brings pages in under these numbers), we feel that QA should be spot-checking and flagging these numbers to the UI team as well.

Other page performance tools include:

  • Google Page Speed
  • PageSpeed Insights (Chrome Add-on)
  • WebPageTest: http://www.webpagetest.org/
  • YSlow (Firefox Add-on)

Testing Responsive Designs

Mobile has turned testing on its ear. The days of safety testing on two or three browsers are well behind us. How can we now reliably test on the never ending arrival of devices that appear on the market on an almost daily basis? Here are a couple of helpful tips:

  1. Support the latest devices and most used browsers only. By embracing progressive enhancement, we are doing our due diligence to support as many of the older devices and browsers as we possibly can. The mobile market is now moving faster than we can hope to keep up with. The latest devices we purchase are nearly obsolete several months after purchasing them. The years to come will bring us even more smartphones, tablets, smart televisions, wearable devices, and other gadgets yet to be conceived.
  2. Test on real devices! Emulators are okay, but will only get you so far and do not always accurately portray a site as it will be experienced on a real phone or tablet. Test on as many devices as you have access to, with the allotted time that you have. Testing responsive sites on desktop browsers is fine, but it must not be the only test you run of site performance on smaller screens.
  3. Pray! (Just kidding...well, maybe not)

Emulation in Chrome DevTools

If you need to test or smokescreen your work in an emulator, look no further than Chrome DevTools.

Important! Emulation should not be used if TMP has a perfectly functioning device on hand to test on. Only use when there are no other options available or time is extraordinarily short. Emulation testing is better than no physical device testing at all!

Responsive TalentBrew

So, what has changed?

Responsive TalentBrew is an ongoing endeavor to improve some of the underlying code that may negatively impact the overall user experience across all viewing environments—especially on mobile devices. These updates, as always, seek to improve the workflow of UI developers as well.

To date, we have seen the following improvements:

  1. Addition of Responsive Flag (When enabled, optimized code will load)
  2. Rearrangement of render-blocking assets, scripts removed from <head> and placed at bottom of page, etc. (this helps perception of faster loading page)
  3. Removal of baked in functionality such as modal windows (these decisions are best left up to the UI and creative teams)
  4. Refactoring of key features such as Advanced Job Alerts, Employee Profiles (Meet Our People), Job Matching, and Work With Me
  5. Framework improvements:
    • Script optimizations and reductions
    • IE conditionals (for older IE support...if needed)
    • Addition of responsive helpers (Modernizr, Viewport meta tags, etc.)
  6. New Features:
    • GeoLocation (a.k.a. GPS)
  7. Server Configurations:
    • Addition of response header to disable Internet Explorers "compatibility mode" on client-side (this alone killed off many complaints about page display in older versions of Internet Explorer)
    • Addition of web.config to Seostatic.tmp.com to manage file types (web wont support coming soon)
  8. Admin Updates:
    • UI can now add scripts to the bottom of the template

Conclusion

Mobile and responsive design and development may seem disruptive at times, and perhaps even a little unsettling. Fear not the future! Responsive should simply be looked at as the maturation of the web and a natural next step in its evolution. By working closely with one another we can overcome the new challenges that are presented and push this medium forward.
